Introduction

Our goal is to understand how a potentially large group of participants can be supported to conduct collaborative online decision-making processes with a least amount of external governing factors. Since there exists a multitude of systems that are regularly used for tasks such as online participation, much of the required functionality is well known. This includes mechanisms for proposing an item, discussing it and voting on alternatives. However, there is one aspect of collaborative online decision-making that is not yet well understood: how can a large group of participants work together to come up with and decide on proposals? In real-world politics, there is a vast array of instruments to refine proposals and form coalitions in order to reach a final decision. Our idea is to take two of the key underlying elements of these instruments and translate them to an online setting. Namely, improving an existing proposal to gather more support for it and forming a coalition to join the support of two or more proposals. We believe that these elements can be translated to mechanisms that are used for collaborative software development through distributed version control systems.

Based on this idea, we have developed decide, an application for collaborative online decision-making that supports the participants to work together to come up with the best possible solution for a given issue. This does not require the participants to have homogenous interests and agree on a solution. It gives them all the tools they need to come up with good proposals, improve them, form coalitions and decide on the result.

We have conducted several experiments using decide. The results of those experiments are encouraging. Participants understand and use the new mechanisms provided for improving and merging proposals. Our experiments also suggest that the specific voting system employed may have a significant impact on how the participants use the option to improve and merge proposals. This opens a very challenging and interesting line of future research.

Features of decide

Our application decide gives participants several ways to participate in a collaborative decision-making process. This includes common functions like creating new proposals and commenting on them. What is unique, however, is that participants have the possibility to create proposals based on other proposals. When creating a new proposal, they are given the choice whether they want to fork (enhance, develop, …) or merge (coalition, compromise, …) existing proposals. Forks and merges are ordinary proposals by themselves, with the exception that they have relations to their parent proposals. They especially do not replace their parents.

As we want to encourage the participants to collaborate, we decided to use a public, iterative voting 1 process. It is possible at any time to see how many other participants have approved the proposals. This also means that the current forerunner is always visible. We believe this is essential for collaboration, because only when participants know the intentions of their peers can they meaningfully improve their proposals.

With this in mind, we support participants at various points in the development of their proposals. For example, if a participant´s preferred proposal is not in the lead right now, they get a prompt to look for a coalition.

Findings from our lab experiments

We tested our idea in two experiments with untrained students. The two processes differed only in their voting procedure. In one, participants could only give one approval at a time (plurality voting) and in the other, as many as they wanted (approval voting).

Through the experiments we discovered advantages and disadvantages with both methods. Plurality voting was deemed unfair by some participants due to vote splitting—a problem that is amplified with public votes. Open research questions

How can we encourage evolution of proposals, while keeping the set of candidate small and relevant?

Since we want to encourage change and therefore progress towards better decisions we naturally increase the total number of proposals that were made, by a far greater amount than a traditional system that allow proposals. As every proposal should be respected, we can not just hide a proposal which has a child that may or may not be an improvement for some. How can we determine a relevance for a given proposal?

How to vote on candidates with relations between them?

Imagine you expressed your approval with a certain proposal. Later on you approve a child of that proposal. How should your vote be counted? Should we prioritize the vote for a newer proposal, because we assume new = better? This may also be important when making a decision with multiple possible winner, as in reality proposals that are very closely related are often not suited to be implemented at the same time. E.g. Add a slide to the playground and*Add a jungle gym to the playground* may be constricted by available space on the playground.

How can we help participants find candidates for a coalition?
